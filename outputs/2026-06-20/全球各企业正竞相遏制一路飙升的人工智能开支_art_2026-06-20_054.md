企业正竞相遏制飙升的人工智能成本

对最重度用户而言，词元极大化正失去吸引力

2026年6月18日

“这会是一场彻头彻尾的噩梦，”一家美国大型科技公司的高管说。他说的是使用人工智能的企业正面临的一个新问题。人工智能代理——能够阅读、解读并采取行动的机器人——会消耗大量算力，并已开始累积巨额账单。随着它们扩散，这个问题还会扩大。这名高管指出，大公司通常使用数百个软件程序。如果其中每一个都提供代理功能——它们很可能会——人工智能成本就可能失控上升。

预算管理是人工智能采用者的新烦恼。不久前，企业还鼓励员工大肆使用这项技术。消耗海量词元——模型处理的文本片段，常被用作计价单位——成了一种荣誉徽章；技术人员称之为“词元极大化”。公司在内部排行榜上展示员工对人工智能的使用情况。Meta的榜单给顶级用户授予“词元传奇”等称号。

这类激励部分解释了人工智能支出的繁荣。企业使用这项技术的方式发生变化，也起了作用。推理模型和代理等词元密集型应用正变得更受欢迎。在某些情况下，代理会构建自己的代理，使成本进一步上升。企业信用卡提供商Ramp分析客户的交易数据，以揭示它们的人工智能使用情况。该公司估算，过去一年客户在这方面的支出增长了13倍。今年4月，优步表示，它在4个月内花完了年度人工智能预算。其他公司也有类似问题。据报道，有一家公司一个月就在人工智能词元上花了5亿美元。OpenAI老板萨姆·奥尔特曼称，客户成本不断攀升是“一个巨大问题”。

眼下，问题仍然集中。支出最高的往往是科技公司，因为它们是早期采用者，也因为人工智能尤其擅长编写软件。Ramp估算，在每名员工人工智能支出最高的前1%客户中，人均月度账单约为7450美元；客户中位数则为11美元。而且，尽管这些大户的人工智能账单与在旧金山雇用一名开发者的成本相比并不高，但与在德里雇用一名开发者相比就很高了。

企业正以各种方式应对。虽然人工智能落后者仍在竞相采用这项技术，但对最重度用户而言，词元极大化已经过时。包括Meta和亚马逊在内的一些公司已取消排行榜。许多公司开始更仔细地考虑模型选择。大量任务并不需要昂贵的尖端模型。帮助企业降低云成本的Archera.ai的阿兰·卡纳指出，在某些情况下，Anthropic的非尖端模型Sonnet，成本可能只有其尖端模型Opus的二十分之一。而中国初创公司月之暗面的开源模型Kimi，成本又可能只有Sonnet的二十分之一。

另一种办法是设置支出上限。优步已将员工在每种编码工具上的词元使用额度限制为每月1500美元。咨询公司Thoughtworks的雷切尔·莱科克说，企业如何分配词元，取决于哪里能从人工智能中创造最大价值。她补充说，这通常意味着公司会把最多词元分配给其核心业务职能，比如科技公司的工程师。

<h1>Companies are scrambling to curtail soaring AI costs  </h1>
<h3>For the heaviest users, tokenmaxxing is losing its appeal</h3>
<h3>June 18th 2026</h3>
<p>“It’s going to be an absolute nightmare,” says an executive at a big American tech company. He is talking about an emerging problem for businesses that use artificial intelligence. AI agents—bots that can read, interpret and act—use masses of processing power and have started to run up huge bills. As they proliferate, the problem will grow. Big companies, the executive points out, typically use hundreds of software programs. If each of those offers agents (as they probably will), AI costs could spiral out of control.</p>
<p>Budget management is a new worry for AI adopters. Not long ago employees were encouraged to binge on the technology. Burning through vast numbers of tokens—the chunks of text that models process, which are often used as a unit of pricing—became a badge of honour; techies dubbed it “tokenmaxxing”. Companies showed off staff’s AI use on internal leaderboards. Meta’s display awarded top users titles like “Token Legend”.</p>
<p>Such incentives partly explain the boom in AI spending. So does a change in the way enterprises use the technology. Token-heavy applications, such as reasoning models and agents, are growing more popular. In some cases agents build their own agents, sending costs higher still. Ramp, a corporate-credit-card provider, analyses its clients’ transaction data to shed light on their AI use. It reckons their spending has risen 13-fold in the past year. In April Uber said that it had spent its annual AI budget in four months. Other firms have similar problems. One reportedly spent $500m on AI tokens in a month. Sam Altman, the boss of OpenAI, has called mounting customer costs “a huge issue”.</p>
<p>For now, the problem is concentrated. The top spenders tend to be tech firms, because they are early adopters and because AI is particularly good at writing software. Ramp reckons that the 1% of clients that spend the most on AI per employee are racking up average monthly bills of about $7,450 per person; the median client pays $11. And although the big spenders’ AI bills are low compared with the cost of hiring a developer in San Francisco, they are high compared with employing one in Delhi.</p>
<p>Companies are responding in various ways. Although AI laggards are still racing to adopt the technology, for the heaviest users tokenmaxxing is out. Some, including Meta and Amazon, have scrapped their leaderboards. Many are thinking more carefully about model choice. Plenty of tasks do not require pricey, cutting-edge models. Aran Khanna of Archera.ai, which helps companies reduce their cloud costs, points out that in some cases Sonnet, a lagging-edge model from Anthropic, can cost a twentieth of what Opus, a leading-edge one, does. And Kimi, an open-source model from Moonshot AI, a Chinese startup, can cost a twentieth of that.</p>
<p>Another approach is spending caps. Uber has limited its employees to $1,500-worth of tokens each month per coding tool. How firms choose to allocate tokens depends on where they can generate the most value from AI, says Rachel Laycock of Thoughtworks, a consultancy. That often means companies apportion the most tokens to their core business function, such as engineers in a tech firm, she adds.</p>
<p>Some software vendors are experimenting with new pricing plans to ease clients’ concern over costs. Intercom, one such supplier, offers a service whereby the customer pays only for queries that are resolved by its IT-support agent. The big cloud providers have launched cost-management services, too, including budgeting tools and ways to triage queries to the most appropriate model.</p>
<p>For the model-makers, the balancing act is trickier. They want their biggest customers to use as many tokens as possible without balking at the cost. Today the cost of providing AI services is subsidised by the labs. Indeed, OpenAI’s plan for winning customers from Anthropic reportedly involves drastic price cuts. But eventually the model-makers will need to turn a profit, which will mean raising prices. Pressure to do so will only intensify later this year if, as expected, Anthropic and OpenAI go public. Their customers, then, can look forward to more jaw-dropping AI bills. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
