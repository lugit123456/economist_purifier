商业｜石油、风能与战火

伊朗战争提振了挪威能源巨头埃奎诺

但它无法把一头日渐老去的现金奶牛变成轻快的新贵

2026年6月18日

近期事件厚待了海湾以外的能源企业，其中包括挪威的埃奎诺，即欧洲最大的油气供应商。通常全球五分之一的石油和液化天然气会经由霍尔木兹海峡运输；该海峡关闭以来，欧洲天然气价格自2月起上涨了40%；截至5月的三个月里，布伦特原油均价为每桶103美元。埃奎诺股价上涨了四分之一（见图表）。

因此，你或许会以为，在6月16日埃奎诺资本市场日上会是一片欢腾；这一天也是该公司在奥斯陆和纽约上市25周年。然而，斯堪的纳维亚式的克制占了上风——而且不只是因为伊朗与美国之间的和平将使价格降温。埃奎诺仍被夹在一门日渐老去的化石燃料业务，和一个雄心受挫的绿色投资组合之间。

挪威大陆架上已发现但尚未开发的油气，是埃奎诺65%产量的来源，其规模在1983年达到峰值。如今多数油田已进入成熟期。为维持产量，未来几年埃奎诺打算把60%的资本开支投在本土。这些资金大多会流向靠近现有管道、油井或工厂的小型储藏，以便在两年半内、且在油价每桶35美元或更低时实现盈亏平衡。埃奎诺还打算提高老油田的采收率。它预计挪威产量将在2027年达到峰值，为每日140万桶油当量，并在21世纪30年代初温和下降至每日130万桶油当量。但许多分析师认为，降幅可能更快。

海外业务或许能抵消本土的衰退。埃奎诺正在提升巴卡良油田的产量，这是巴西一个位于厚盐层之下的大型油田；类似资产Raia预计将在2028年前投产。该公司还收购了美国马塞勒斯页岩盆地的资产。

埃奎诺预计，到2030年，国际产量将增长30%，达到每日95万桶油当量。但指望海外项目改变其命运，或许想错了。花旗集团的阿拉斯泰尔·赛姆怀疑，埃奎诺最大股东挪威政府并不赞成全球冒险主义。该公司正避开新国家和前沿勘探，宁愿提升现金流。它预计到2030年，国际业务将贡献90亿美元，增幅为80%。

它的未来会是绿色的吗？该公司2018年更名（由Statoil改为现名）释放了向这个方向转变的信号。埃奎诺曾打算到2030年让可再生能源占其产出的10%，并将2021年至2026年间230亿美元资金指定用于绿色投资。但疫情之后，零部件成本飙升和供应链阻滞使项目超出预算，与此同时，利率上升又推高了融资成本。许可审批延迟和地缘政治不确定性进一步挤压回报。目标市场的增长也慢于预期。可再生能源业务的亏损不断累积。

自2022年以来，埃奎诺已收缩阵线，去年支出不到30亿美元。它退出了一些项目，出售资产，并减记14亿美元。该公司保留了三个海上风电巨型项目，预计这些项目到明年将实现现金流为正。它预计到2030年，其发电量将增长四倍，达到20太瓦时，但主要来自现有项目。

埃奎诺意识到，这也许无法让投资者心潮澎湃，于是承诺给他们很多钱。它打算在2026年将股票回购规模翻倍至30亿美元，并把年度回购维持在20亿至40亿美元，同时每年把股息提高5%以上。伊朗带来的意外之财眼下有所助益，但若没有价格的持久抬升或新的增长，这样的派息很快就需要靠借债来支撑。埃奎诺正安顿下来，成为一头中年现金奶牛。■

<h1>The Iran war has boosted Equinor, Norway’s energy giant </h1>
<h3>But it cannot turn a greying cash cow into a sprightly upstart</h3>
<h3>June 18th 2026</h3>
<p>RECENT EVENTS have been kind to energy firms outside the Gulf—including Norway’s Equinor, the largest supplier of hydrocarbons to Europe. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz, through which one-fifth of the world’s oil and liquefied natural gas (LNG) usually flows, has pushed European gas prices up by 40% since February; Brent crude averaged $103 a barrel in the three months to May. Equinor’s share price is up by a quarter (see chart).</p>
<p>So you might have expected cheer at Equinor’s capital-markets day on June 16th, the 25th anniversary of the company’s listing in Oslo and New York. Yet Scandinavian restraint prevailed—and not just because peace between Iran and America will calm prices. Equinor remains caught between a greying fossil-fuel business and a green portfolio with clipped ambitions.</p>
<p>Discovered but undeveloped oil and gas on the Norwegian continental shelf, the source of 65% of Equinor’s output, peaked in 1983. Most fields are now mature. To sustain production, in the next few years Equinor intends to deploy 60% of its capital expenditure at home. It will mostly go into small deposits near existing pipelines, wells or plants, to break even within two and a half years and at $35 a barrel or less. Equinor also aims to boost recovery from older fields. It expects Norwegian output to peak in 2027 at 1.4m barrels of oil equivalent per day (boe/d), declining gently to 1.3m boe/d in the early 2030s. But many analysts reckon it may drop faster.</p>
<p>Operations abroad may offset decline at home. Equinor is ramping up Bacalhau, a big Brazilian field under a thick layer of salt; Raia, a similar asset, is expected online by 2028. The firm has also bought assets in America’s Marcellus shale basin.</p>
<p>Equinor expects international production to grow by 30% by 2030, to 950,000 boe/d. But hopes that foreign ventures will transform its fortunes may be misplaced. Alastair Syme of Citigroup doubts that the Norwegian government, Equinor’s largest shareholder, favours global adventurism. The firm is avoiding new countries and frontier exploration, preferring to boost cashflows. It expects $9bn, or 80% more, from international operations by 2030.</p>
<p>Could its future be green? The firm’s renaming (from Statoil) in 2018 signalled a shift that way. Equinor intended renewables to make up 10% of its output by 2030, earmarking $23bn for green investments from 2021 to 2026. But after the pandemic, surging costs for components and supply-chain snags drove projects over budget, just as higher interest rates made financing dearer. Permitting delays and geopolitical uncertainty further squeezed returns. Target markets also grew more slowly than expected. Losses from renewables mounted.</p>
<p>Since 2022 Equinor has scaled back, spending less than $3bn last year. It has quit projects, sold assets and written off $1.4bn. The firm retains three offshore-wind mega-projects, which it expects to be cashflow-positive by next year. It expects its power production to grow fourfold to 20 terawatt-hours by 2030, but mostly from existing projects.</p>
<p>Conscious that this may fail to enthrall investors, Equinor is promising them lots of money. It intends to double share buybacks to $3bn in 2026, and to keep them at $2bn-4bn a year, while raising its dividend by more than 5% a year. The Iran windfall helps for now, but without a lasting bump in prices or new growth, such payouts will soon require borrowing. Equinor is settling down as a middle-aged cash cow. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
