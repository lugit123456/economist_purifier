## 📊 中文解读

### 🌟 一句话核心主旨
特朗普第二任期给美国邻国带来贸易、能源与安全冲击，却意外强化了拉丁美洲作为投资目的地的吸引力。2025年该地区FDI升至估计2040亿美元、股市指数自2025年初涨60%，但这种红利依赖美国政策热情，且受暴力风险、财政脆弱与结构瓶颈制约。

### 🔍 核心观点与论据拆解
- 特朗普第二任期令拉美不安：他扰乱贸易、推高能源价格，并试图在西半球施压；原文称其绑架委内瑞拉领导人、向加勒比派炮舰并威胁入侵古巴，但混乱也带来资本流入。
- 2025年特朗普重返白宫第一年，拉美FDI逆转下滑、升至估计2040亿美元，而其他发展中地区资金流萎缩；M&A几乎增长一半，MSCI拉美股市指数自2025年初上涨60%。
- 拉美宏观环境改善支撑投资：除阿根廷和玻利维亚外，里奥格兰德以南33国均实行浮动汇率或盯住美元；通胀目标制央行和财政改革吸引资本，2005至2019年GDP年均增长2.8%。
- 美国官员称需用替代融资削弱中国影响；拉美拥有全球已探明铜储量逾三分之一、许多可行稀土矿床，且石油出口多于进口。2025年FDI增量至少一半来自美国，UN贸易官员作此判断。
- 矿业受益最明显：2025年1月至9月拉美占全球矿业M&A支出四分之三。美国与阿根廷、巴拉圭、智利达成交易，并向Serra Verde贷近6亿美元，随后一家美国稀土矿企以30亿美元收购该公司。

### 🤨 争议与潜在挑战
原文呈现的核心争议在于，美国支持既是机遇也是风险。美国官员认为，通过开发融资与供应链建设可稀释中国影响，特朗普也相信美国应与其想影响的地区建立更紧密经济联系；投资者因此押注拉美居首。相反，文章指出特朗普任期仅剩两年半，注意力短、脾气更短，热情可能消退，若再次诉诸暴力会吓退部分投资者。地区内部旧疾也未消失：巴西财政受难以负担的养老金拖累，阿根廷依赖IMF平衡账目，墨西哥GDP在2026年第一季度收缩；基础设施短缺、熟练工不足和官僚主义也压低前景。Aclara Resources的Ramon Barúa Costa称，美国政府给这家成立五年的巴西矿企500万美元虽有帮助，但若把部分业务迁往美国可获得更多支持，并更接近研究人员和加工化学品，其下一座矿石分离设施将设在路易斯安那，显示超级大国支持可能是双刃剑。

### 🔮 未来趋势预判
若特朗普政府继续推动拉美供应链、资源安全与替代融资，矿业、药品、金融科技等领域仍可能吸引外资，尤其铜、锂、稀土、钛、铀等资源相关项目。与此同时，中国与欧盟也会被美国压力激发行动：BYD正在巴西扩张，智利曾在2026年1月考虑由中国企业建设通往香港的海底光缆；欧盟同月与南方共同市场四国签署酝酿25年的自贸协议，Bloomberg Economics估计可最终使四国合计GDP提高0.7%。

<h1>Does Donald Trump make Latin America a good bet?</h1>
<h3>Nowhere in the developing world has done so well out of the past year</h3>
<h3>June 18th 2026</h3>
<p>FOR AMERICA’S neighbours, Donald Trump’s second term has been unsettling. As well as disrupting trade and sending energy prices soaring—bad news almost wherever you are—Mr Trump is determined to impose his will on the western hemisphere. He has kidnapped the leader of Venezuela, sent gunboats to the Caribbean and threatened to invade Cuba.</p>
<p>Yet Latin America is also benefitting from Trumpian chaos. In 2025, Mr Trump’s first year back in office, foreign direct investment (FDI) rose to an estimated $204bn, reversing a recent decline (see chart 1), while flows to the rest of the developing world shrank. Mergers and acquisitions (M&amp;A) were up by nearly half. An index tracking Latin American stock markets from MSCI, which compiles such things, has risen by 60% since the start of 2025, beating emerging markets as a whole for most of that period (see chart 2).</p>
<p>It helps that Latin America is no longer a place of unstable currencies and spiralling inflation. All 33 countries south of the Rio Grande, save Argentina and Bolivia, float their currencies or peg them to the dollar. Inflation-targeting central banks and fiscal reforms have slowly lured investors. Between 2005 and 2019 the region’s GDP grew, on average, by a steady 2.8% a year. Pharmaceuticals, fintech and mining have all attracted foreign money.</p>
<p>That is not wholly coincidental. Mr Trump believes that America should have closer economic ties with places it wants to influence. Latin America, investors reckon, tops the list. To take the most brazen example, in October his Treasury extended a $20bn swap line to Argentina to help Javier Milei, the president and a Trump ally.</p>
<p>American officials say that alternative sources of finance are necessary to dilute China’s influence. Latin America has lots of sought-after minerals, including more than a third of the world’s proven reserves of copper and many of the most viable deposits of rare earths. It also exports more oil than it imports.</p>
<p>At least half of last year’s increase in FDI came from America, according to UN trade officials. A little came from its government itself, some from businesses friendly with the administration, the rest from other private investors. In March 2024 the Development Finance Corporation, America’s foreign-lending agency, set up its first office on the continent in São Paulo. At a dinner for investors in a swanky steakhouse in the city, conversation glided between speculation over where the most promising industries are and where Mr Trump could next use force.</p>
<p>Mining—already experiencing a worldwide investment boom—has been a clear beneficiary. Between January and September 2025 Latin America accounted for three-quarters of the industry’s spending on M&amp;A. This year America has struck deals with Argentina, where lithium reserves are mostly untapped; Paraguay, a source of lithium, titanium and uranium; and Chile, the world’s largest producer of copper. It lent nearly $600m to Serra Verde, a Swiss-registered firm developing a Brazilian deposit of ionic clay, a source of rare earths. An American rare-earth miner part-owned by the federal government then acquired the company for $3bn. Others have bet on deposits ranging from gold in Peru to tantalum in Venezuela.</p>
<p>Established giants have spent the biggest sums. The urgency of American officials to secure resources has led several of them to fast-track existing projects, says a mining boss. In March Australia’s BHP, which runs the world’s biggest copper mine, in Chile, announced a $5bn expansion. According to an American official, more than $5bn will be spent in Chile in 2026 to get resources out of the ground in 2027 alone. Another large miner is considering reserving the proceeds of some new mines solely for American customers.</p>
<p>Mr Trump may be provoking non-Americans into action—not least the Chinese, whose enthusiasm had cooled after the pandemic. BYD, a carmaker, is expanding operations in Brazil. In January Chile said it was considering an undersea fibre-optic cable to Hong Kong, to be built by a Chinese firm. American officials were sufficiently outraged to ban from America the Chilean politician overseeing the cable.</p>
<p>The European Union has also strengthened economic ties. In January it signed a free-trade deal with four members of Mercosur, a South American trading bloc. Argentine and Brazilian ranchers, who produce beef more cheaply than EU farmers, will be among the winners. The agreement had been in the works for 25 years, but Mr Trump’s tariffs prodded policymakers to get it over the line. According to Bloomberg Economics, a research firm, the deal could eventually boost the Mercosur quartet’s combined GDP by 0.7%.</p>
<p>The good times are by no means sure to last. Investors’ interest is partly based on hope that Mr Trump means to build supply chains in Latin America, as his officials claim. But Mr Trump has two and a half years left in office, a short attention span and a shorter fuse. His enthusiasm could fizzle. And if he resorts to violence again, some investors would be scared away.</p>
<p>Despite recent progress, the region’s economies still contend with old problems. The finances of Brazil, the biggest, are weighed down by unaffordable pension costs. Argentina relies on the IMF to balance its books. And Mexico’s GDP contracted in the first quarter of 2026. Add a shortage of infrastructure, a lack of skilled workers and stifling bureaucracies, and the picture looks altogether gloomier.</p>
<p>Ramon Barúa Costa of Aclara Resources, a five-year-old Brazilian mining firm that has received $5m from the American government, says that, helpful as that is, he could get more by moving some operations to the United States—and also be closer to researchers and chemicals for processing. Aclara’s next ore-separating facility will be in Louisiana. A superpower’s support can be a mixed blessing. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
