### 🌟 一句话核心主旨
美伊停战谅解备忘录令能源市场松一口气，但霍尔木兹海峡清雷、油轮回流、保险与伊朗收费等障碍仍会限制供应。战前每桶60美元的充裕石油时代短期难返，布伦特虽从5月逾110美元跌破80美元，却仍可能在数月内高位震荡。

### 🔍 核心观点与论据拆解
- 谅解备忘录要求30天内解除伊朗港口海上封锁并重开霍尔木兹海峡，全球可望恢复通常15-20%的石油和液化天然气供应。伊朗需重启出口，特朗普也希望11月中期选举前降低汽油价格。
- 市场已押注和平，布伦特从5月逾110美元跌至80美元以下，部分分析师预计明年供给恢复快于需求、出现油市过剩。但Argus Media的Tom Reed称，谨慎买家尚未大量订购海湾原油，生产、炼油和航运恢复都需时间。
- 最大短期瓶颈是清雷。《经济学人》看到的IRGC地图显示水雷布在通常航道，伊朗与阿曼沿岸替代航线狭窄危险。美国有扫雷船，英国和法国愿协助伊朗残余海军；多数船东或等六周以上，Kpler估计海湾内118艘满载油轮正常需10-15天离港，清雷中会更久。
- 供应恢复将分阶段推进。Gunvor的Frédéric Lasserre称，沙特和阿联酋等较轻原油大油田三个月内可回战前产量。多数分析师预计海湾总产量7月中达2月水平30-50%，9月中达60-70%，年底达80-90%，布伦特或趋近75美元。
- 船运和政策摩擦会延长紧张。BRS称入港油轮完全回归需四五个月；约50艘最大原油船只能装两周战前中东出口量。Howden的Ellis Morley称，保险费须等航行自由获保障且大运营商连续数周通行后才会下降。

### 🤨 争议与潜在挑战
乐观方认为停战将带来供应恢复，国际能源署6月17日预计明年中东增产和其他新活跃产油方不停产将造成油市过剩。但摩根士丹利预计第三季度仍短缺340万桶/日，全球库存以210万桶/日速度下降，7-9月「dated Brent」均价90美元、随后三个月80美元，均比2月预测高20美元。伊朗承诺60天不收通行费，却可能改收费用；4月伊朗央行确认PGSA首笔收入，议会法案拟将30%收入分给包括IRGC在内的武装力量。若和平协议不取消美国对PGSA和IRGC制裁，付款者有被列入黑名单风险。精炼品更难判断：伊朗打击损坏海湾大型炼厂，非洲汽油、亚洲石脑油和LPG、欧洲航空燃油和柴油库存可能被消耗；美国炼厂或转向国内汽油，但库存低可能令泵价维持高位甚至上涨。

### 🔮 未来趋势预判
即便真正停火达成，供应仍受船运、清雷、保险、伊朗收费和制裁风险制约；美国若取消下一次战略库存释放，还会少掉100万桶/日出口，而夏季需求可能更快回升。到明年若出现过剩，全球库存此前也可能触及纪录低位；若各国补充或新建战略储备，2027年全球需求或增加200万桶/日，中国此前已削减原油进口500万桶/日、约占全球供应5%，可能率先补库。Rystad Energy估计，伊朗重新控制霍尔木兹或战事复燃的风险，可能给全球油价长期增加最高10美元/桶溢价。

<h1>Deal or no deal, oil prices will stay volatile for months</h1>
<h3>The pre-war days of $60 crude are not coming back soon</h3>
<h3>June 18th 2026</h3>
<p>IT IS A moment of relief for energy markets. A <a href="https://www.economist.com/middle-east-and-africa/2026/06/15/a-deal-is-only-the-beginning-of-the-end-of-the-us-iran-war">memorandum of understanding</a> to end the war between America and Iran calls for lifting the naval blockade of Iranian ports and reopening the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days. While Donald Trump and his Iranian counterparts work out the details of a peace deal, the world can look forward to recovering 15-20% of its usual supply of oil and liquefied natural gas.</p>
<p>It may all still be derailed by skirmishes or disagreements over the fine-print. Yet both sides have incentives to stop fighting. Iran’s wrecked economy needs oil exports to resume; Mr Trump wants cheaper petrol ahead of the midterms in November. Markets are pricing in peace. Brent crude, the global benchmark, has slid below $80 a barrel, from well over $110 in May. Some analysts expect an oil glut next year, as supply recovers faster than demand.</p>
<p>The optimists may be getting ahead of themselves—especially in the short term. Wary buyers are not yet placing large orders for Gulf crude, notes Tom Reed of Argus Media, a price-reporting agency. Even if the deal holds, tankers must start returning to the Gulf, not just leaving it, production must restart and refining ramp up worldwide—all of which will take time.</p>
<p>Before any of that, the strait must first be cleared of mines. A map from the IRGC, Iran’s elite fighting force, seen by The Economist, suggests they have been laid precisely where ships usually sail. Alternative lanes, along the Iranian and Omani coasts, are dangerous and narrow. Few ships brave them (see chart 1). America has mine-clearing vessels in the region, and Britain and France have offered to help what is left of Iran’s navy.</p>
<p>Intrepid vessels will attempt passage before then. One Greek shipowner says he will do so shortly. A few insurers are cutting top-up premiums for Gulf voyages by 50% for some clients, says Argus. Mercuria, a Swiss trader; a Vietnamese refiner; and a Chinese importer are looking for vessels to lift Gulf oil in the next week. But most shipowners may wait for mines to be fished out, which could take six weeks or more. The 118 laden tankers now idling in the Gulf would normally need 10-15 days to exit, reckons Kpler, a ship-tracker. With demining in process this will take longer.</p>
<p>As tankers return, Gulf producers will start pumping. They have reported no severe damage to fields. Most have enough spare storage to raise output before maritime bottlenecks are fully resolved. Large fields with lighter crude, such as Saudi and Emirati ones, should reach pre-war production within three months, says Frédéric Lasserre of Gunvor, a trader.</p>
<p>Most analysts expect overall Gulf output to reach 30-50% of February levels by mid-July, 60-70% by mid-September and 80-90% by the end of the year. In this scenario Brent would edge towards $75 a barrel. Prices in the Gulf and outside it, which diverged when the strait was first shut, are converging again (see chart 2).</p>
<p>Still, the pre-war world of abundant oil is a long way off. Even if outbound vessels start crossing the strait, inbound ones may not return in full for four or five months, predicts BRS, a ship broker. The 50 or so of the biggest crude carriers waiting near the Gulf can load only about two weeks’ worth of pre-war Middle Eastern exports. Many ships that migrated to safe and lucrative Atlantic routes must complete their voyages and will wait for Gulf freight rates to rise before heading back. Insurers will lower premiums only when freedom of navigation is guaranteed and big operators have been sailing Hormuz for weeks, says Ellis Morley of Howden, an insurance broker.</p>
<p>Although Iran has promised no “tolls” for 60 days, it may charge “fees” instead. In April its central bank confirmed receipt of the first revenue from the new Persian Gulf Strait Authority (PGSA). A bill allocating 30% of the proceeds to Iran’s armed forces, including the IRGC, is in parliament. Iran’s recent communications suggest it views managed traffic as the new normal and unrestricted passage as the aberration, says someone familiar with the matter. Yet unless the peace deal lifts American sanctions on the PGSA and the IRGC, anyone paying Iran risks being blacklisted. </p>
<p>Supply will, then, stay constrained even after a real truce is reached. On top of that, traders expect America to cancel its next release from emergency stocks, depriving the world of 1m b/d in exports. Demand, which normally rises in the summer, may return faster on news of the war’s end. Morgan Stanley anticipates an oil deficit of 3.4m b/d in the third quarter, draining global stocks at a rate of 2.1m b/d and keeping prices high. The bank forecasts “dated Brent” (oil to be delivered in the next few weeks) will average $90 between July and September and $80 in the three months after. That is $20 more than it predicted in February for the same periods.</p>
<p>The prices of refined products are even harder to predict. Asian refiners must wait for Gulf supplies to arrive. In the Gulf itself, Iranian strikes have damaged big refineries. Further delays to product shipments could drain stocks in Africa (starved of petrol), Asia (a big importer of naphtha, a plastics feedstock, and LPG, a cooking fuel) and Europe (reliant on Gulf jet fuel and diesel). American refiners may swing from producing diesel and jet fuel for export back to petrol for the home market—but domestic stocks are so low that prices at the pump may stay high, or even rise.</p>
<p>On June 17th the International Energy Agency, an official forecaster, said it was expecting an oil glut next year, as the Middle East starts pumping more and newly active producers elsewhere do not stop. But global oil stocks may hit record lows before then (see chart 3). If governments decide to refill their strategic reserves—or, for those without such stocks, to build them—this may add 2m b/d to global demand in 2027. China, which has cut crude imports by 5m b/d—roughly 5% of global supply—may be first in line.</p>
<p>And even if supply does start to outstrip demand, the risk that Iran reasserts control over Hormuz—or that the fighting resumes—may add a lasting premium of up to $10 a barrel to world oil prices, reckons Rystad Energy, a consultancy. For energy markets, the end of the war does not mean the end of uncertainty. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
