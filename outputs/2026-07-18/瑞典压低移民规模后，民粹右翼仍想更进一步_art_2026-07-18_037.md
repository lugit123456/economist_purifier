### 🌟 一句话核心主旨
瑞典已通过极严移民政策把年度净移民从2016年峰值117000人降至去年12000人，但反移民的瑞典民主党仍推动从限制入境转向促使已在境内者离开。9月13日大选将检验瑞典选民是回归自由、国际化的主流政治，还是给予民粹右翼更大执政角色。

### 🔍 核心观点与论据拆解
- 斯德哥尔摩附近Stora Essingen长期支持温和党，但自2022年温和党与强硬反移民的瑞典民主党达成权力分享协议后，选民转向中左翼社会民主党，希望阻止瑞典民主党进入下一届政府。
- 瑞典民主党称已驯服或驱逐最极端成员，党徽也从燃烧火炬变成蓝黄花。2022年它成为议会最大右翼党，支持温和党领袖Ulf Kristersson组阁，获得政策影响但无部长职位；Kristersson如今承诺若连任将给其内阁职位。
- 瑞典的超限制移民政策受到欧洲许多右翼赞赏。社会民主党在2010年代中期欧洲难民危机期间收紧庇护规则和边境检查；瑞典难民法律中心NGO的Louise Dane称，早期改革是降低瑞典吸引力，现政府重点则是让已持居留者离开。
- 政府目标是遣返融合不佳或失业移民，移民部称从伊拉克、索马里等国返回人数已多于抵达人。瑞典民主党移民发言人Ludvig Aspling说他们希望很多人离开，认为不贡献者没有理由留在瑞典。
- 自1月起，难民可申领最高350000瑞典克朗，即36000美元，返回原籍国，金额为英国类似计划的9倍，但预计每年仅600人接受。瑞典民主党称若削减福利人数会增加，并希望扩展至入籍公民。

<h1>Sweden squashed migration. The populist right wants to go further</h1>
<h3>The Sweden Democrats could be heading for a bigger role in government</h3>
<h3>July 16th 2026</h3>
<p>NOT FAR from central Stockholm lies Stora Essingen, a sleepy island covered with lush trees and pretty houses. For years its residents voted overwhelmingly for the Moderate Party, Sweden’s mainstream conservatives. But since 2022, when the party entered a power-sharing pact with the hard-right anti-immigrant Sweden Democrats (SD), voters have signalled their displeasure at its alliance with the hitherto pariah.</p>
<p>Instead voters have shifted towards the centre-left Social Democrats, whose activists are knocking on doors ahead of the general election on September 13th, in the hope that it can keep the SD out of the next government. Many voters in places like Stora Essingen view their country as a sensible, liberal and cosmopolitan democracy. The election will test whether that idea of Sweden wins out—or whether, having had a taste of anti-immigrant populism, Swedes want more of it.</p>
<p>The SD argues that it has tamed (or expelled) its most extreme elements. Its logo, once a blazing torch, is now a blue-and-yellow flower. In the election in 2022, amid anger over a surge in migration a few years earlier, it became the biggest right-wing party in parliament and backed a government headed by Ulf Kristersson, the leader of the Moderates; the SD got policy influence, but no ministerial jobs. Today Mr Kristersson promises the SD cabinet roles if the coalition is re-elected.</p>
<p>The election will reveal whether the SD’s popularity can endure once migration slows to a trickle. Sweden’s hyper-restrictive policies—admired by much of the European right—have reduced annual net migration from 117,000 at its peak in 2016 to 12,000 last year (see chart).</p>
<p>The Social Democrats tightened asylum rules and border checks while in power during Europe’s refugee crisis in the mid-2010s. “The first wave of changes tried to make Sweden less attractive to people trying to come here,” explains Louise Dane of the Swedish Refugee Law Centre, an NGO. But as the number of arrivals fell and Mr Kristersson’s government took over, the focus shifted. “Now the policy changes are about trying to get people who are already here with residency permits to leave.”</p>
<p>The government aims to repatriate poorly integrated or out-of-work migrants. The migration ministry boasts that more people are returning to countries such as Iraq and Somalia than are arriving from them. Atervandring (repatriation) is a watchword for the SD. “We want a lot of people to leave,” says Ludvig Aspling, the party’s migration spokesman. “There’s no reason to have people here from other countries if they’re not contributing.”</p>
<p>To that end the government is encouraging migrants to go. Since January refugees have been able to claim up to SKr350,000 ($36,000) to return to their country of origin—nine times what a similar scheme offers in Britain. Only 600 people a year are expected to take it. The SD argues this would increase if welfare benefits were trimmed. It wants to expand the scheme to naturalised citizens, too.</p>
<p>A “good behaviour” law, passed last month, allows the government to deport migrants for infractions such as unpaid debts. It has tightened family-reunification rules and stopped issuing permanent residence to refugees. The SD also wants to downgrade those who already hold this status to temporary residence. </p>
<p>A chunk of voters likes these plans. But as migration has dropped, the SD’s popularity has remained stuck at around 20%. The party’s main issue is losing its salience. Last year just 23% of Swedes named integration as one of their biggest concerns, compared with 54% in 2015, according to surveys by the University of Gothenburg’s SOM Institute. That could help the Social Democrats, who struggle to convince voters they are tough on migration. </p>
<p>For the right, this shift has made politics awkward. Last month public outrage caused the government to ease rules that had led to the deportation of teenagers who grew up in Sweden. Business groups have begun complaining that they cannot get foreign talent. The Liberal Party, part of Mr Kristersson’s coalition, opposes the SD’s push to revoke permanent residency.</p>
<p>Some voters who tolerated the SD’s rougher edges when migration was a bigger worry may find them harder to ignore today. On July 5th Richard Jomshof, a senior SD politician, said: “It is no more strange to hate Islam than it is to hate national socialism”. Some fear that talk like this risks emboldening ugly extremes (something the SD strenuously denies).</p>
<p>Populist-right parties across Europe face a challenge of staying relevant even as rates of migration slow. Many will be watching this election to see if the SD’s pivot—to trying to remove migrants who have already arrived—will be as popular with voters as the previous wave of anti-immigrant policies. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
