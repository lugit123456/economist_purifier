### 🌟 一句话核心主旨
《经济学人》认为，各国投资名人的建议虽共享应急储蓄、长期投资、防骗局等基本常识，却往往映照并回应本国金融文化的偏差：美国怕债务、英国怕投资、印度和韩国迷恋短线交易。

### 🔍 核心观点与论据拆解
- 个人理财建议曾只由高薪专业人士服务富人；自1980年代起，美国广播和电视上的名人顾问扩大受众，如今社交媒体finfluencers面向全球发声。文章认为，投资导师既可能抵制受众恶习，也可能无意强化它们。
- 美国代表是65岁的Dave Ramsey，由电台明星转为播客主持人。他强调应急基金、理性储蓄和长期买股票，但对债务近乎清教徒式排斥，甚至建议已存退休金或子女教育金者尽快还清房贷，包括2022年前取得的低息贷款。
- Ramsey的严厉立场回应美国人超支倾向。即便经济增长强劲，许多家庭仍因负债陷入困境。2026年第一季度，13%的信用卡余额逾期90天或以上，接近全球金融危机后2010年的历史高位。
- 英国权威理财名人是Money Saving Expert创始人Martin Lewis，其强项是超市优惠、开户优惠、换银行或公用事业供应商等省钱技巧。但他也反映英国人厌恶投资：英国家庭金融资产中股票仅占13%，几乎为大型发达经济体最低；美国为44%。
- 亚洲理财网红更常研究个股。印度Rachana Phadke Ranade是特许会计师，YouTube有540万粉丝，谈IPO、板块、商品和每月股票；韩国3PRO TV有300万订阅者，每天多条视频，关注Samsung、SK Hynix等芯片巨头，并售卖投资课程。

### 🤨 争议与潜在挑战
原文指出，Ramsey让听众提前还高息消费债是合理的，但把低息房贷也一律要求尽快还清则过于僵硬。Martin Lewis的省钱建议实用，却可能无法弥补英国人错过长期复利收益的问题。对印度和韩国，原文承认关注短期市场走势本身并非错误，但对多数投资者而言，频繁交易股票、尤其是衍生品，和储蓄太少或只顾省小钱一样危险。印度上一财年约850万人交易高风险股票衍生品，八年前不足100万人；印度监管机构研究显示，其间多数时候十个散户中九个亏钱。韩国小投资者gaemi推动并追逐股市繁荣，4月至6月股票投资贷款累计达405亿美元纪录，半导体行情波动后许多投机者承压。

### 🔮 未来趋势预判
文章暗示，随着社交媒体finfluencers继续扩张，各国理财建议会更鲜明地贴合本国金融弱点：美国继续围绕债务纪律，英国需要从省钱转向投资，印度和韩国则面临遏制杠杆短炒冲动的挑战。稳健理财原则像托尔斯泰笔下幸福家庭一样相似，但各国金融罪过各有不同，因此未来理财建议的风险也会呈现本土化差异。

<h1>What investment gurus get wrong</h1>
<h3>To see a country’s financial follies, look to its celebrity advisers</h3>
<h3>July 16th 2026</h3>
<p>FINANCIAL ADVICE was once doled out solely by well-paid professionals, and solely to the wealthy. Then, from the 1980s, celebrity advisers took their tips to large radio and TV audiences, especially in America. Today legions of social-media “finfluencers” spout off recommendations to anyone, anywhere.</p>
<p>As in previous decades, the recommendations of investment gurus—how to cut expenses, build a savings fund or avoid scams—are sensible and universal. Beyond the shared basics, though, their counsel differs in telling ways. They may not know it, but they hold up a mirror to their countries’ financial mores,  resisting audiences’ vices or accidentally reinforcing them.</p>
<p>In America no young finfluencer yet matches the stature of Dave Ramsey, a gruff 65-year-old radio star turned podcaster. Much of his advice—offered in a stern, fatherly style—is evergreen, built around emergency funds, sensible saving and buying shares for the long term.</p>
<p>Mr Ramsey’s guidance, however, is puritanical in its attitude to debt. Sensibly, he urges listeners to pay off high-interest consumer debts early. But he also suggests that anyone already saving for retirement or children’s education should pay off their mortgages as fast as possible, too—even low-interest loans secured before 2022.</p>
<p>The rigid advice is in part a reaction against Americans’ proclivity to spend, spend, spend beyond their means. Even in periods of strong economic growth many households find themselves in financial distress through debt. In the first quarter of 2026, 13% of credit-card balances were 90 days or more overdue, close to the record high from 2010, following the global financial crisis.</p>
<p>Britain’s undisputed heavyweight financial guru is Martin Lewis, the founder of “Money Saving Expert”, a website. He channels a different failing: an aversion to investment. Mr Lewis’s stock-in-trade is practical penny-pinching: supermarket bargains, introductory offers and the benefits of switching banks or utility providers. By his own account, until recently he barely touched the stock market. Only in the past year has he turned his attention to Britain’s individual savings accounts for shares, a generous scheme that allows people to invest up to £20,000 ($26,700) per year without incurring the usual investment taxes.</p>
<p>The attitude of Mr Lewis reflects the bleak reality that many Brits own barely any equities. A measly 13% of the financial assets of British households are shares, the lowest in almost any large developed economy. In Ramseyan America the figure is 44%. No amount of scrimping and saving will compensate Britons for missing out on the profound benefits of long-term compound returns.</p>
<p>Asian personal-finance sages often pepper advice with research on individual stocks. Rachana Phadke Ranade, a chartered accountant with 5.4m YouTube followers, is one of India’s most prominent finfluencers. Her videos go beyond staid book-keeping counsel into initial public offerings, stock-market sectors and commodities. Subscriber-only videos focus on stocks of the month. South Korea’s 3PRO TV, a YouTube channel with 3m subscribers, likewise publishes several videos a day, often on market-moving news about the country’s chipmaking giants, Samsung and SK Hynix. It also sells its own investment seminars.</p>
<p>The advice reflects the financial culture, rather than the other way round. South Koreans and Indians (at least those with financial resources) save more than Americans or Brits, but suffer from a zeal for day-trading, often backed by leverage. About 8.5m Indians traded risky equity derivatives in the last financial year, up from fewer than 1m eight years ago. A study by Indian regulators suggested that for much of that time, nine in ten retail traders have lost money. South Korea’s stock-market boom has been chased—and driven—by an army of small investors, known as gaemi (ants). Between April and June the cumulative loans taken out to invest in stocks hit a record $40.5bn, and many punters are now facing pain as the semiconductor rally turns volatile.</p>
<p>There is nothing inherently wrong with following short-term market movements. But for most investors, constantly trading shares, let alone derivatives, is as bad an idea as saving too little or prioritising penny-pinching over compound returns. The rules of sound finances are, like Leo Tolstoy’s happy families, alike everywhere. Financial sins are sinful in their own ways—and so it is with financial advice. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
