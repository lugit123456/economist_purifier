### 🌟 一句话核心主旨
美国在印太地区被视为日益不可靠，正推动印度、澳大利亚、日本、印尼、新西兰等亚洲中等强国加强彼此合作，以在中美两大超级大国阴影下寻求战略保险。莫迪近期密集外交显示，这种靠拢并非为了抛弃美国或围堵中国，而是在旧分歧之上寻找可共同推进的防务、海上安全和技术议程。

### 🔍 核心观点与论据拆解
- 莫迪外交行程异常密集：上月访问法国和斯洛伐克，是其自2014年上任以来第100次出访；回国后又赴塞舌尔，在德里接待日本首相高市早苗，随后前往印尼、澳大利亚和新西兰。
- 这些印太活动体现亚洲中等强国寻求保险政策。莫迪公开推进防务、海上安全、半导体和稀土协议，背景是中国的经济分量与领土野心，以及美国可靠性下降使地区平衡更艰难。
- 印度外交分析人士C. Raja Mohan称莫迪路线为「G minus 2」，对应特朗普有时谈及与中国达成的所谓G2。其观点是，亚洲国家既不能背弃美国，也不能遏制中国，只能搁置旧怨、探索彼此能做什么。
- 美国变化令印度尤其不适。印度虽已摆脱特朗普去年8月施加的「惩罚性关税」，但在其任内，少有人认为印美关系可修复。6月美国把「印太司令部」改回「太平洋司令部」，也被德里视为冷落。
- 印度与澳大利亚、日本、新西兰和印尼合作升温。7月9日澳大利亚同意向印度供应核能用铀，并协调海上安全；印日首次同意防务生产，项目是无线电天线；新西兰谈更多海军演习和情报共享；雅加达签署印度向印尼供应BrahMos导弹系统协议。

### 🔮 未来趋势预判
基于原文事实，亚洲中等强国的密集外交预计会持续，尤其会围绕防务生产、海上安全、信息共享、关键技术和供应链展开。由于这些国家不打算背离美国，也无法遏制中国，未来合作更可能是务实、分领域、渐进式推进，而非形成正式同盟。

<h1>An unreliable America is drawing Asia’s middle powers closer</h1>
<h3>A burst of Indian diplomacy exemplifies the trend</h3>
<h3>July 16th 2026</h3>
<p>NARENDRA MODI is often seen pressing flesh at bilateral meetings, or embracing his favourite counterparts with a bear hug. A visit to France and Slovakia last month was the peripatetic Indian prime minister’s 100th foreign trip since taking office in 2014. Shortly after returning to India, he jetted off to the Seychelles. He was then in Delhi to host Japan’s prime minister, Takaichi Sanae, before heading to Indonesia, Australia and New Zealand.</p>
<p>The flurry of activity in the Indo-Pacific illustrates a trend: Asia’s middle powers want an insurance policy. Mr Modi aired deals on defence, maritime security, semiconductors and rare earths—all in the shadow of the two superpowers. Like other Asian countries, India has long had to contend with the economic heft and territorial ambitions of China. But as America has become less reliable the balancing act has got rather harder.</p>
<p>C. Raja Mohan, an Indian foreign-policy analyst, has described Mr Modi’s approach as “G minus 2”, referring to the so-called G2 pact that Donald Trump at times talks of striking with China. Asian countries are not turning their backs on America or seeking to contain China, he says: they know they cannot do either. Instead, they are working out what they can do together, putting aside old differences.</p>
<p>For India the shift in America’s position has been particularly jarring. Though it has managed to get off the “punishment tariffs” Mr Trump imposed last August, few see how it can repair its relations with America while he is in office. Diplomats elsewhere in the region are aghast at the deterioration of such a vital relationship.</p>
<p>Such worries were further aggravated in June when America said that it was rebranding its Indo-Pacific Command, as it terms its military structure in the region, as the Pacific Command. The small textual change carried a big message. “Indo” had been added in 2018 to signal maritime co-operation between countries in the Quad—America, Australia, India and Japan—and portray India in particular as a critical partner. Its removal was seen as a snub in Delhi and another sign of American retrenchment.</p>
<p>What did Mr Modi’s tour yield? Most promising is a convergence between India and Australia. Their relationship has long been constrained by Australia’s opposition to nuclear proliferation and India’s suspicion of Western alliances. Politicians from Australia’s Labor Party have also raised concerns about Kashmir. Such differences are now forgotten. On July 9th Australia agreed to supply India with uranium for nuclear energy. Notably, the two will also co-ordinate action on maritime security. “India has long resisted that kind of language,” says Ian Hall of Griffith University.</p>
<p>India’s ties with Japan are also getting stronger, with the focus shifting from infrastructure to defence and technology. The two have agreed to work together on defence production for the first time, even if their inaugural project (making radio antennae) is small-bore. In New Zealand Mr Modi spoke of plans for more naval exercises and information sharing. In Jakarta he signed a deal to supply Indonesia with India’s BrahMos missile system.</p>
<p>Mr Modi is “essentially implementing Mark Carney’s idea of middle powers coming together in Asia”, says Ajay Bisaria, a former Indian high commissioner to Canada. Yet for all his frenetic activity, mutual concerns will not inevitably lead to deep co-operation. Asia’s middle powers still differ on many issues, especially over how to handle Mr Trump, and in some cases  attitudes towards China. </p>
<p>Compared with those in Europe, Asian countries may be more divided over how to respond to a shifting balance between the two superpowers. But they are also a lot more exposed. Expect the energetic diplomacy to continue. <span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
