## 📊 中文解读

### 🌟 一句话核心主旨
凯文·沃什接任美联储主席后，试图缩减因2007-09年全球金融危机、2009-2014年三轮QE及2020年疫情QE而膨胀至6.7万亿美元、近GDP 21%的资产负债表，但银行体系已深度适应充裕准备金环境，安全退出极其困难。

### 🔍 核心观点与论据拆解
- 美联储资产负债表扩张源于QE：先买抵押贷款支持证券稳住房市，后买长期国债刺激经济。如今规模达6.7万亿美元，国债约占三分之二，沃什批评其代表美联储对经济影响力扩张。
- 沃什组建任务小组，成员包括哈佛大学Karen Dynan、印度央行前行长Raghuram Rajan、前美联储理事Jeremy Stein。核心问题不是是否想缩表，而是能否在不破坏金融体系的情况下安全降低水位。
- 反对大资产负债表的理由包括：QE若有效，是通过扭曲长期债务市场发挥刺激作用；长期持有低息债券在利率上升期代价高昂，因为美联储需按浮动利率向银行准备金付息，却只能收取疫情期锁定的超低债券收益。
- 负债端约束决定缩表空间：流通货币约2.5万亿美元不能缩；财政部在美联储账户约8000亿美元略有弹性；最大变量是准备金，已从金融危机前约100亿美元升至约3.1万亿美元，必须从银行体系抽走。
- 银行体系已围绕充裕准备金重塑。危机前准备金不付息，银行会在联邦基金市场出借多余准备金；QE后美联储通过准备金付息控制短端利率，流动性监管又要求银行自备流动资产，导致大额准备金缓冲成为常态。

### 🤨 争议与潜在挑战
原文保留多方分歧。经济学家仍争论QE是否真正有效；沃什主要担忧制度形象，认为持有数万亿美元国债会让美联储看似美国财政部的钱袋。斯坦福大学Darrell Duffie则称，目前批评多是观感问题，但若不缩表，观感未来可能变成现实。技术挑战也很突出：2017-2019年首次缩表时，美联储逐步降准备金，但2019年9月准备金短缺、隔夜利率飙升；去年准备金接近低水位时压力再现，美联储从12月中旬至4月中旬以每月约400亿美元速度购买国库券，反而再度扩表。政策方案也有争议：Stephen Miran主张把准备金利率压到低于联邦基金市场收益；Duffie建议升级美联储支付软件，在结算前抵消收付款，类似英格兰银行和欧洲央行系统；Rajan甚至提出可让危机中第一家机构倒下，以约束银行在充裕准备金下过度冒险。

### 🔮 未来趋势预判
各方都承认调整需要时间，沃什也承认，一个扩张18年的资产负债表不可能用超过18周就缩小。若直接抽干准备金风险过高，美联储更可能先改变资产构成，即让长期债券到期后改持短期国库券，从而减少对长期借贷成本的影响。这样不会真正降低资产负债表规模，但可能缓解美联储过度介入经济的观感。

<h1>How to shrink the Fed’s $7trn balance-sheet</h1>
<h3>Kevin Warsh will struggle to reverse the effects of bond-buying</h3>
<h3>July 16th 2026</h3>
<p>IMAGINE A CITY that, after years of drought, floods a river valley and turns it into a vast reservoir. In the decade that follows, houses spread across once-barren hillsides, factories rise where water now flows freely and farmers trade their wells for irrigation canals. Soon, the city is built around abundant water. Then comes a new mayor—an irritable NIMBY who regards the whole expanse as an eyesore. What is the mayor to do?</p>
<p>Kevin Warsh, the Federal Reserve’s new chairman, faces a similar dilemma. During the global financial crisis of 2007-09 the Fed first began buying mortgage-backed securities to steady the housing market, and later long-dated Treasuries to stimulate the broader economy—a policy known as quantitative easing (QE). Three rounds of QE between 2009 and 2014, then another during the covid-19 pandemic in 2020, have swelled the Fed’s balance-sheet to $6.7trn, or nearly 21% of GDP. Treasuries account for about two-thirds of its holdings. Ever suspicious of central-bank sprawl, Mr Warsh fumed last year that this was “a proxy for the Fed’s growing imprimatur on the economy”.</p>
<p>In pursuit of a lower waterline Mr Warsh has assembled a task-force of engineers: Karen Dynan of Harvard University, Raghuram Rajan, an ex-head of India’s central bank, and Jeremy Stein, a former Fed governor. Can the task be done safely?</p>
<p>There are several objections to the Fed’s large balance-sheet. If QE works as a tool of macroeconomic stimulus—a question economists still debate—it does so by distorting the market for long-term debt. If the intervention lasts, so will the distortion. Holding long-term bonds has also proven costly during a period in which interest rates have risen. The Fed pays commercial banks a floating rate of interest on the electronic money it creates to buy bonds, known as “reserves”, which are a liability on its balance-sheet. Yet the yields the Fed earns on its bonds are locked in when they are bought—during the pandemic, at ultra-low levels.</p>
<p>Mr Warsh’s main concern, however, is institutional. Although the Fed buys government bonds in secondary markets rather than directly from the Treasury, owning trillions of dollars’ worth can still make it look like Uncle Sam’s piggy bank. For now, notes Darrell Duffie of Stanford University, “these criticisms are mostly about perceptions”—more eyesore than menace.</p>
<p>To limit the risk that, in Mr Duffie’s words, the perceptions “someday become reality”, the balance-sheet must shrink. The assets cannot decline unless liabilities do, too, so the question is how much room there is on the other side of the ledger. Currency in circulation makes up about $2.5trn of the Fed’s liabilities and cannot be shrunk. The Treasury’s deposit account at the Fed adds another $800bn or so and may offer some wriggle room. The largest liability is reserves, which have risen from around $10bn before the global financial crisis to about $3.1trn today. For the balance-sheet to shrink, these must be drained from banks.</p>
<p>The trouble is that just as the city was gradually moulded around abundant water, the banking system is now built around abundant reserves. Before the financial crisis, reserves paid no interest, so a bank with more than it needed would try to lend them to others and earn the short-term interest rate prevailing in the interbank, or “federal funds” market. As QE flooded the system, the Fed kept control of short-term rates by paying interest on reserves, reducing banks’ incentive to lend them to others. New liquidity regulations pushed in the same direction: for example, banks must show that they can meet their needs using their own liquid assets. Large reserve buffers have thus become the norm.</p>
<p>Banks also adapted their businesses to the new regime. When the Fed bought Treasuries from non-bank investors, the proceeds often landed in those investors’ bank accounts as uninsured deposits. This funding in turn allowed banks to expand their investment portfolios and extend more credit. Mr Rajan says this leads to the “ratcheting” effect: each round of QE left banks dependent on a higher level of reserves.</p>
<p>As with draining a reservoir, judging how low the level can now fall without causing collapse is hard. During its first attempt to unwind QE, between 2017 and 2019, the Fed reduced reserves gradually, hoping to leave enough for the financial system to function. But in September 2019 reserves ran short and overnight interest rates spiked. Strains reappeared last year, as reserves neared the low-water mark. The Fed responded by buying Treasury bills at a pace of around $40bn a month from mid-December to mid-April, expanding its balance-sheet again to top up the reserve pool.</p>
<p>Stephen Miran, another former Fed governor and a critic of balance-sheet bloat, wants the Fed to cut the interest rate paid on reserves below what banks could earn by lending them in the federal-funds market. Mr Duffie favours updating Fed software to offset incoming and outgoing payments before they are settled, allowing banks to operate with smaller buffers (the Bank of England and European Central Bank have such systems). Mr Rajan has mused about the Fed letting the first institution caught in a crisis topple, as a way to discipline banks from taking too much risk in a world where abundant reserves encourage credit growth.</p>
<p>Everyone agrees that any change will take time. Even Mr Warsh has acknowledged that a balance-sheet expanded over 18 years will take “more than 18 weeks” to shrink. Rather than drain the reservoir, he may settle for changing its composition. This could mean replacing long-dated bonds with short-term Treasury bills as they mature, which would reduce the Fed’s influence over longer-term borrowing costs while leaving the balance-sheet’s size unchanged. That would not lower the waterline. But it might improve the view.<span class="span_ufinish">■</span></p>
